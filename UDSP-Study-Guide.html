Present Tenses

📌 Present Simple — Facts, Habits, Routines
  • Used for things that are generally true — facts, scientific truths, permanent situations.
  • Used for habits and routines — things that happen regularly, not just "right now."
  • Used for schedules and timetables that are fixed.
  • Common mistake: using Present Continuous for habits just because the sentence is in the present — habits always take Present Simple.
  • Frequency adverbs (always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, never) are a strong signal for this tense, placed before the main verb (but after "to be").
fact Water boils at 100°C at sea level.
habit She goes to the gym every morning before work.
routine The 8:15 train leaves from platform 3.
frequency adverb He rarely complains about his workload.
permanent situation My parents live in a small town near the coast.
📌 Present Continuous — Now, Temporary, Changing
  • Used for actions happening at this exact moment.
  • Used for temporary situations — true only "around now," not permanently.
  • Used for describing a trend or change currently in progress.
  • Also used for fixed future arrangements (see below).
  • Key contrast with Present Simple: "I work in marketing" = permanent job; "I'm working on a new campaign this week" = temporary activity.
  • Stative verbs (know, believe, want, need, like, own, belong) describe states, not actions — they are NOT normally used in continuous form.
right now Please be quiet — the baby is sleeping.
temporary She normally works from the office, but this month she's working from home.
trend/change More and more people are choosing to work remotely these days.
✗ wrong I am knowing the answer. (stative verb — never use continuous)
✓ correct I know the answer.
📌 Present Perfect — Connecting Past to Now
  • Links a past action/state to the present moment, without specifying exactly when it happened.
  • Used for life experiences with no fixed time — "I have visited Japan."
  • Used with "since" + starting point or "for" + duration, to show something continuing up to now.
  • Used with "already," "yet," "just," "ever," "never" for recent or unspecified past events.
  • Used when the result of a past action is still relevant now.
  • Biggest trap: if a specific time is mentioned (yesterday, in 2020, last week), you MUST use Past Simple, not Present Perfect.
life experience I have visited Paris twice. (no specific time)
since She has worked here since 2020.
for They have lived in this city for ten years.
already/yet Have you finished the report yet? — Yes, I've already finished it.
result relevant now I have lost my keys. (I still don't have them)
✗ wrong I have visited Paris in 2019. (specific time → must be Past Simple)
✓ correct I visited Paris in 2019.
📌 Present Perfect Continuous — Emphasising Duration
  • Used for an action that started in the past and is still continuing now.
  • Also used when an action has just stopped but has a visible/felt effect on the present.
  • Focus is on the duration or ongoing nature, not the result.
  • Compare: "I have written three reports today" (Present Perfect — focus on completed result, how many) vs "I have been writing reports all day" (Present Perfect Continuous — focus on the ongoing, tiring activity).
still ongoing They have been negotiating the contract for three weeks.
visible effect now I'm exhausted because I have been studying all night.
duration emphasis It has been raining non-stop since this morning.
result emphasis (contrast) I have written five emails this morning.
📌 Present Continuous for Future Arrangements
  • Used for fixed, already-arranged future plans.
  • Usually involves another person, a booking, or a specific confirmed time.
  • Different from "going to," which expresses intention without a confirmed exact arrangement.
I'm meeting the dentist at 5 PM tomorrow. (already booked)
We're flying to Istanbul next Friday. (tickets already bought)
💡 Exam tip: Three quick checks for present-tense gap-fills: (1) is there a specific past date? → not Present Perfect. (2) is there "since/for/already/yet/just/ever/never"? → likely Present Perfect. (3) is it a stative verb (know, want, belong)? → never use continuous form.
✏️ Check Yourself

1) The sun - - - - in the east — this never changes.

2) I can't talk right now — I - - - - dinner.

3) She - - - - three different countries, but she's never been to Asia.

4) My brother - - - - a new car — he's wanted one for years. (state verb)

5) We - - - - for over an hour, and the bus still hasn't arrived.

Past Tenses

📌 Past Simple — Finished Actions at a Specific Time
  • Used for a completed action or state at a definite point in the past.
  • The time may be stated explicitly (yesterday, in 2020, last week) or understood from context.
  • Used for a sequence of completed past actions, one after another.
  • Used for past habits or states that are no longer true (often with "used to").
  • Key requirement: the action must be seen as finished — not ongoing, not open-ended.
specific time She graduated from university in 2019.
sequence of actions He woke up, made coffee, and left for work.
past habit, no longer true When I was a child, I played football every weekend.
implied time (from context) A: Did you call her? B: Yes, I called her this morning.
📌 Past Continuous — Action in Progress in the Past
  • Describes an action in progress at a specific moment in the past.
  • Often interrupted by a shorter action, which takes Past Simple.
  • Used for two simultaneous ongoing actions in the past.
  • Used to set the background scene of a story before the main event.
  • Typical pairing: Past Continuous (background, longer action) + Past Simple (interruption, shorter action), connected by "when" or "while."
interrupted action I was cooking dinner when the phone rang.
two simultaneous actions While she was driving, he was checking the map.
background/scene-setting It was raining heavily, and the streets were almost empty, when the accident happened.
✗ wrong While she drove to work, it started to rain. (the longer, background action should be continuous)
✓ correct While she was driving to work, it started to rain.
📌 Past Perfect — The "Earlier Past"
  • Used when already talking about the past, to refer to something that happened even earlier — before that past point.
  • Establishes a clear sequence: Past Perfect for the earlier event, Past Simple for the later one.
  • Common trigger words: by the time, before, after, when, already, never...until, no sooner...than.
  • One of the most frequently tested structures — many learners use Past Simple for both events and lose the sense of sequence.
by the time By the time we arrived, the film had already started. (film started first)
before She had finished the report before her manager asked for it.
after After he had left the office, he realised he'd forgotten his keys.
when When I got there, everyone had already gone home.
never... until She had never seen snow until she moved to Canada.
✗ wrong By the time she arrived, the meeting started. (no clear sequence shown)
📌 Past Perfect Continuous — Duration Before a Past Point
  • Emphasises how long an action had been going on before another point in the past.
  • Works the same way Present Perfect Continuous does, but shifted to the past.
  • Often explains the cause of a later past situation.
She had been waiting for over an hour when the bus finally arrived.
They had been working on the project for months before it was cancelled.
His eyes were red because he had been crying.
📌 Used to vs Would (Past Habits)
  • Both describe repeated past actions that are no longer true.
  • "used to" can also describe past states (not just actions).
  • "would" is only for repeated actions, never for states.
action — both possible We used to / would go camping every summer.
state — only "used to" There used to be a cinema on this street. (NOT "would be")
💡 Exam tip: When two past events are mentioned, always ask "which happened first?" If one clearly happened before the other, the earlier one needs Past Perfect, not Past Simple.
✏️ Check Yourself

1) By the time the ambulance arrived, the situation - - - - already under control.

2) I - - - - dinner when the lights suddenly went out.

3) She was tired because she - - - - all night for the exam.

4) There - - - - a small shop on this corner, but it closed years ago.

5) He - - - - his keys, his wallet, and finally his phone before he could leave the house.

Future Forms

📌 Will — Predictions and Spontaneous Decisions
  • Used for predictions based on opinion or general belief (no strong evidence needed).
  • Used for spontaneous decisions made at the moment of speaking.
  • Used for promises and offers.
  • Used for facts about the future we have no control over.
  • The most "neutral" future form — used when there's no prior plan, just an expectation or in-the-moment choice.
prediction/opinion I think it will rain later.
spontaneous decision It's cold in here — I'll close the window.
promise I will call you as soon as I land.
offer Don't worry, I'll help you carry those boxes.
fact, no control She will turn 30 next month.
📌 Going to — Plans and Evidence-Based Predictions
  • Used for intentions/plans decided before the moment of speaking.
  • Used for predictions based on strong, visible evidence right now.
  • Contrast with "will": will = decided now/opinion; going to = decided earlier/clear evidence.
prior plan We're going to renovate the kitchen next year.
evidence now Look at those dark clouds — it's going to rain.
intention He's going to apply for the manager position.
📌 Present Continuous for Fixed Arrangements
  • Used when there's a fixed, confirmed arrangement — booking, appointment, meeting.
  • Usually involves a specific time, place, or another person.
  • Slightly "more certain" than "going to" — implies confirmation already in place.
I'm meeting the dentist at 5 PM tomorrow.
We're flying to Istanbul next Friday.
✗ less natural I think it's raining tomorrow. (weather isn't a fixed arrangement)
📌 Present Simple for Timetables and Schedules
  • Used for future events that are part of a fixed timetable — trains, flights, official calendars.
  • Not a personal arrangement, but something officially fixed and unlikely to change.
The train leaves at 9 AM tomorrow.
The conference starts on the 14th and ends on the 16th.
📌 Future Perfect — Completed Before a Future Point
  • Describes something that will be finished before a specific point in the future.
  • Commonly used with "by," "by the time," or "before."
By next June, she will have graduated from university.
By the time you get this message, I will have already left.
📌 Future Perfect Continuous — Duration up to a Future Point
  • Emphasises the duration of an action still ongoing up to a future point.
By the end of this year, he will have been working here for a decade.
In one more hour, we will have been driving for twelve hours straight.
📌 Future Continuous — Action in Progress at a Future Time
  • Describes an action that will be in progress at a specific moment in the future — like Past Continuous, shifted to the future.
This time tomorrow, I will be lying on a beach.
Don't call at 8 PM — we will be having dinner then.
💡 Exam tip: Ask: is there visible evidence or a prior plan (→ going to), a fixed booking (→ present continuous), an official schedule (→ present simple), or just an opinion/spontaneous choice (→ will)?
✏️ Check Yourself

1) Look at the score — Brazil - - - - win this match easily. (clear evidence now)

2) The flight - - - - at 7:45 according to the schedule.

3) By 2030, scientists - - - - a cure for many more diseases.

4) I'm already - - - - my sister at the airport — we confirmed it yesterday.

5) It's cold in here — I think I - - - - the heater on. (decided right now)

Conditionals (Part 1): Zero, First, Second

📌 Zero Conditional — General Truths and Facts
  • Describes things that are always true — scientific facts, natural laws, general cause-and-effect.
  • Doesn't depend on a specific time.
  • Both clauses use Present Simple.
  • "If" can usually be replaced with "when" or "whenever" without changing the meaning, since the result is automatic and certain.
If you heat ice, it melts. (scientific fact)
If prices rise, demand usually falls. (general principle)
When/If you mix red and blue, you get purple.
📌 First Conditional — Real, Likely Future Situations
  • Talks about realistic, possible situations in the future and their probable results.
  • The "if" clause uses Present Simple (NOT "will," even though it's about the future).
  • The main clause uses "will" + base verb.
  • Describes something that could genuinely happen — a real possibility worth planning for.
  • Other modals (can, may, might, should) can replace "will" for different functions (permission, advice).
If it rains tomorrow, we will postpone the trip.
If you don't hurry, you will miss the bus.
with "can" If you finish early, you can leave. (permission)
with "should" If you feel unwell, you should see a doctor. (advice)
✗ wrong If it will rain tomorrow, we will postpone. ("will" never appears in the if-clause)
📌 Second Conditional — Unreal Present or Future
  • For hypothetical, unlikely, or imaginary situations in the present or future.
  • Things that are not true now and probably won't happen, but we imagine them anyway.
  • The "if" clause uses Past Simple (a grammatical signal of "unreality," not a real past tense).
  • The main clause uses "would" + base verb.
  • With "to be," correct (and more formal) usage is "were" for ALL subjects — "If I were you..." not "If I was you..."
If I won the lottery, I would travel around the world.
If she had more free time, she would learn a new language.
"were" for all subjects If I were you, I would accept the offer.
If he were taller, he could play professional basketball.
📌 First vs Second Conditional — How to Choose
  • The choice is about how LIKELY you consider the situation, not objective truth.
  • The same real-world situation can be expressed with either, depending on the speaker's attitude.
First — feels likely If I get the promotion, I will buy a new car.
Second — feels unlikely If I got the promotion, I would buy a new car. (speaker doubts it will happen)
💡 Exam tip: If the "if" clause has Present Simple, expect "will" in the result (First — real). If the "if" clause has Past Simple, expect "would" (Second — unreal) — never mix "will" into an "if" clause.
✏️ Check Yourself

1) If you - - - - water below 0°C, it freezes.

2) If she - - - - the bus, she'll be late for the interview.

3) If I - - - - a million dollars, I would buy a house by the sea.

4) If I - - - - you, I wouldn't trust him so quickly.

5) If you heat metal, it - - - -.

Conditionals (Part 2): Third, Mixed, Inverted & Wish

📌 Third Conditional — Unreal Past
  • Describes a past situation that did NOT actually happen, and the imagined result if it had.
  • Purely hypothetical — both the condition and the result are contrary to what really happened.
  • Structure: If + had + V3, ... would/could/might + have + V3.
  • One of the most heavily tested structures — requires controlling two "have" auxiliaries at once.
  • Common mistake: defaulting to Second Conditional forms instead.
If they had left earlier, they would have caught the train. (in reality, they missed it)
If she had studied harder, she might have passed the exam. (in reality, she failed)
If we had known about the traffic, we could have left earlier.
✗ wrong If they left earlier, they would catch the train. (Second Conditional — wrong for a past situation)
📌 Mixed Conditionals
  • Combine a condition from one time period with a result in a different time period.
  • Most common: a past condition + present result.
  • Also possible: a general/permanent trait + past result.
  • Describe how an unreal past action still affects the present, or how a permanent characteristic affected something in the past.
past condition → present result If she had taken that job offer, she would be living abroad now.
past condition → present result If I hadn't missed the flight, I would be at the conference right now.
general trait → past result If he were more organised, he wouldn't have missed the deadline.
📌 Inverted Conditionals (Formal Style, No "If")
  • In formal writing, "if" can be dropped and replaced by inverting the subject and auxiliary.
  • Common with "were," "had," and "should."
  • Recognising this structure is essential — without "if," it can look confusing at first.
Were she to apply, she would have an excellent chance. (= If she applied...)
Had the engineers tested it more carefully, the failure could have been avoided. (= If the engineers had tested...)
Should you need any help, don't hesitate to call. (= If you should need any help...)
📌 Wish + Past Simple — Desire About the Present
  • Expresses a desire for something to be different right now, even though it cannot be.
  • Similar logic to Second Conditional — past tense marks unreality, not real past time.
I wish I spoke French fluently. (I don't, but I want to)
She wishes she had more free time.
📌 Wish/If Only + Had + V3 — Regret About the Past
  • Expresses regret about something in the past that cannot be changed.
  • Very similar in logic to Third Conditional.
If only I had listened to her advice. (I didn't, and now I regret it)
I wish I hadn't said that to him.
📌 Wish + Would — Wanting Someone Else to Change
  • Expresses frustration or a desire for someone else's behaviour to change.
  • Cannot be used about yourself.
  • Often carries a sense of complaint or irritation.
I wish he would stop interrupting during meetings.
I wish the neighbours would turn the music down.
📌 Unless, Provided that, As long as, On condition that
  • All introduce a condition, similar to "if (not)."
  • Common in more formal contexts.
Unless you book early, tickets will sell out. (= if you don't book early)
You can borrow the car provided that you return it by 6 PM.
As long as you finish the report today, the deadline is fine.
The bank approved the loan on condition that he provided a guarantor.
💡 Exam tip: If you see "Were/Had/Should" at the very start of a sentence with no "if," check whether removing the inversion and adding "if" makes sense — this confirms an inverted conditional.
✏️ Check Yourself

1) If the engineers - - - - the bridge more carefully, the accident could have been prevented.

2) If I hadn't stayed up so late last night, I - - - - so tired right now.

3) I wish I - - - - more time to prepare for the interview yesterday.

4) I wish my colleague - - - - arriving late every single day.

5) - - - - you finish the assignment by Friday, you'll be allowed to join the trip.

Passive Voice — All Forms

📌 Why and When We Use Passive Voice
  • Shifts the focus from WHO performs an action to WHAT the action is done to.
  • Used when the doer is unknown, unimportant, obvious, or deliberately left out.
  • Common in formal, scientific, or news writing.
  • Structure: always be + past participle, with "be" changing form by tense.
  • Test: is the subject DOING the action, or RECEIVING it? If receiving, passive is required.
active The committee approved the new budget. (committee = doer)
passive The new budget was approved by the committee. (budget = receiver)
passive, doer unknown My phone was stolen last night.
passive, doer obvious The suspect was arrested yesterday.
📌 Passive Across All Tenses
  • The passive structure can appear in any tense.
  • Only the form of "be" changes; the past participle stays the same.
Present Simple The reports are reviewed every Monday.
Present Continuous The building is being renovated this month.
Present Perfect The documents have been reviewed already.
Past Simple The bridge was built in 1932.
Past Continuous The building was being renovated last year.
Past Perfect The contract had been signed before the deadline.
Future Simple A decision will be made next week.
Future Perfect The project will have been completed by June.
📌 Passive with Modal Verbs
  • Structure: modal + be + past participle.
  • Extremely common in formal, rule-based, or instructional sentences — laws, regulations, manuals.
The contract must be signed by both parties.
The results should be announced by Friday.
All visitors can be asked to show identification.
This issue might have been avoided if testing had been done properly.
📌 Passive with Two Objects (Indirect Passive)
  • Some active sentences have two objects (a person and a thing).
  • Either object can become the subject of the passive sentence.
  • Making the PERSON the subject is more common and natural.
active The company gave her a bonus.
passive (more natural) She was given a bonus.
passive (less common) A bonus was given to her.
📌 Passive with "by" and "with"
  • "By" introduces the agent (who/what performed the action).
  • "With" introduces the instrument or material used.
  • Both are optional and often omitted if not important.
The window was broken by the storm. (agent)
The door was opened with a key. (instrument)
📌 Get + Past Participle (Informal Passive)
  • In informal English, "get" sometimes replaces "be" to form the passive.
  • Often used for unplanned or sudden events.
My car got damaged in the storm. (informal for "was damaged")
He got fired last week.
💡 Exam tip: For passive questions, identify the real subject of the action first. If the sentence's grammatical subject is the receiver (not the doer) of the verb, the passive form is almost always required.
✏️ Check Yourself

1) The new policy - - - - by the board next Monday.

2) This report - - - - right now, so please don't disturb the team.

3) All employees - - - - to attend the safety training.

4) The window was broken - - - - a falling branch.

5) The mistake - - - - if the report had been checked twice.

Modal Verbs & Reported Speech

📌 Modals of Obligation: Must vs Have to
  • Both express strong obligation, but the source differs.
  • "Must" = obligation from the speaker's own judgement/feeling (internal).
  • "Have to" = obligation from an external source — law, rule, authority.
  • In the negative, meanings split completely: "mustn't" = prohibition (forbidden); "don't have to" = no obligation (optional).
must — speaker's own feeling I must call my mother today — I haven't spoken to her in weeks.
have to — external rule Employees have to wear safety helmets on site.
mustn't — prohibition You mustn't smoke in this building.
don't have to — no obligation You don't have to attend if you're busy.
📌 Should / Ought to — Advice and Expectation
  • Express advice, recommendation, or what is expected/likely (not a strict obligation).
  • "Should" is far more common in everyday and exam English.
  • "Ought to" is more formal, slightly less frequent, but means the same.
You should see a doctor about that cough.
The package ought to arrive by Friday.
You shouldn't leave your laptop unattended in a café.
📌 Modals of Deduction (Certainty About the Present)
  • Express how certain we are about something happening NOW, based on evidence.
  • must = strong positive certainty.
  • can't = strong negative certainty (logically impossible).
  • might/may/could = uncertain possibility.
She must be exhausted — she's been working for sixteen hours straight.
That can't be right — the shop closed an hour ago.
He might be stuck in traffic.
📌 Modals of Deduction About the Past: must/can't/might have + V3
  • The same certainty logic applies to the past, combined with "have + past participle."
  • One of the most tested modal structures in advanced exams.
She must have left already — her coat is gone.
He can't have finished the report — he only started an hour ago.
They might have missed the train.
She could have called to let us know. (criticism — she had the option but didn't)
📌 Reported Speech — Backshift of Tenses
  • When reporting what someone said, tenses generally shift one step further into the past.
  • Present → Past  |  Past Simple → Past Perfect  |  Present Perfect → Past Perfect  |  "will" → "would."
Direct: "I am tired." → Reported: She said she was tired.
Direct: "I have finished the report." → Reported: He said he had finished the report.
Direct: "I will call you." → Reported: She said she would call me.
Direct: "I went there yesterday." → Reported: He said he had gone there the day before.
📌 Reported Questions
  • Lose the original question word order (no auxiliary inversion) and lose the question mark.
  • Become normal statement word order.
  • "Yes/No" questions are introduced with "if" or "whether."
Direct: "Where do you live?" → Reported: He asked where I lived. (not "did I live")
Direct: "Are you coming to the party?" → Reported: She asked if/whether I was coming to the party.
Direct: "What time does the shop close?" → Reported: He wanted to know what time the shop closed.
📌 Reported Requests and Commands
  • Usually expressed with to + infinitive (positive commands).
  • Or not to + infinitive (negative commands/prohibitions).
Direct: "Please close the door." → Reported: She asked me to close the door.
Direct: "Don't be late." → Reported: He told us not to be late.
Direct: "Sit down!" → Reported: The teacher told the students to sit down.
📌 Subjunctive-Style Structures After Insist/Suggest/Recommend
  • After verbs like "insist," "suggest," "recommend," "demand" (expressing a strong wish), a base-form structure or "should + base verb" is often used in formal English.
  • Applies regardless of the subject.
The manager insisted that the report must be finished by Friday.
The doctor recommended that he rest for a few days.
She suggested that we should leave earlier to avoid traffic.
💡 Exam tip: For deduction modals, decide first whether you're talking about NOW (must/can't/might + base verb) or the PAST (must/can't/might + have + past participle) — mixing these up is one of the most common mistakes.
✏️ Check Yourself

1) You - - - - wear a seatbelt in this country — it's the law.

2) She - - - - already left — the lights in her office are off.

3) Direct: "Where is the nearest station?" → Reported: He asked where - - - -.

4) Direct: "Don't touch that." → Reported: She told the child - - - - it.

5) He - - - - taken the earlier train, but he overslept.

Relative Clauses & Connectors

📌 Relative Pronouns: who, whom, which, whose, where, when
  • Connect a clause to a noun mentioned earlier, adding information about it.
  • who/whom → people (who = subject, whom = object, though "who" is common for both informally).
  • which → things.
  • whose → possession (regardless of whether the owner is a person or thing).
  • where → places.
  • when → time.
who The engineer who designed the bridge later won an award.
whom The man whom I met yesterday is the new director.
which The report, which took weeks to prepare, was finally approved.
whose The director, whose latest film won several awards, gave a short speech.
where The town where she grew up has changed completely.
when I still remember the day when we first met.
📌 Defining vs Non-Defining Relative Clauses
  • Defining (restrictive) clauses give essential information — without it, the sentence would be unclear; no commas.
  • Non-defining (non-restrictive) clauses add extra information that could be removed; always separated by commas.
  • Key rule: "that" can replace "who/which" in defining clauses, but NEVER in non-defining clauses.
defining — essential The man who called you is my uncle. (tells us WHICH man — no commas)
non-defining — extra My uncle, who lives in Berlin, called me yesterday.
"that" in defining The book that I borrowed was excellent.
✗ wrong My uncle, that lives in Berlin, called me. ("that" cannot be used in non-defining clauses)
📌 Omitting the Relative Pronoun
  • In defining clauses, the relative pronoun can be omitted when it acts as the OBJECT of the clause.
  • It cannot be omitted when it is the subject.
can omit (object) The book (that) I borrowed was excellent.
✗ cannot omit (subject) The man called me is my uncle. (must be: The man WHO called me...)
📌 Although / Even though vs Despite / In spite of
  • All four express contrast/concession, but different grammar follows.
  • Although / Even though + full clause (subject + verb).
  • Despite / In spite of + noun, pronoun, or -ing form — never a full clause directly.
  • "Despite the fact that" is an exception that DOES allow a full clause.
Although Although it was raining heavily, the event continued as planned.
Despite Despite the heavy rain, the event continued as planned.
Despite + the fact that Despite the fact that it was raining, the event continued.
✗ wrong Despite it was raining heavily, the event continued.
📌 Because / Since / As vs Because of / Due to / Owing to
  • Because, since, as introduce a full clause explaining a reason.
  • Because of, due to, owing to + noun phrase, not a full clause.
  • "Since" and "as" are more formal and common at the start of a sentence.
Because the roads were icy, the school closed early.
Since she had no further questions, the meeting ended early.
Because of the icy roads, the school closed early.
✗ wrong Due to the roads were icy, the school closed. ("due to" can't take a full clause)
📌 Subject-Verb Agreement: Neither/Either, Each/Every, Collective Nouns
  • With "neither...nor" and "either...or," the verb agrees with the noun closest to it.
  • "Each" and "every" always take a singular verb, even for many people.
  • Collective nouns (team, family, government) can take singular OR plural depending on emphasis (one unit vs individual members).
Neither the manager nor the employees were informed.
Either the students or the teacher has to submit the form.
Every employee is required to attend.
The team is playing well this season.
📌 So...that / Such...that — Result Clauses
  • Introduce a result caused by an extreme degree of something.
  • so + adjective/adverb + that
  • such + (a/an) + adjective + noun + that
  • "so" goes before an adjective/adverb; "such" goes before a full noun phrase.
The lecture was so complicated that hardly anyone understood it.
It was such a complicated lecture that hardly anyone understood it.
📌 Participle Clauses (Reduced Clauses)
  • A participle phrase at the start of a sentence must logically refer to the SUBJECT of the main clause.
  • Otherwise it creates a "dangling participle" error.
Having spent years researching the disease, the scientist announced a breakthrough.
✗ wrong Having spent years researching the disease, the funding was finally approved. (funding didn't research — illogical)
📌 Comparatives in Parallel Structures
  • The + comparative..., the + comparative... shows two things changing proportionally together.
The more experiments they ran, the more confident they became.
The sooner we start, the sooner we'll finish.
💡 Exam tip: When you see a comma followed by "which/who/whose," check whether removing that clause changes the core meaning — if not, it's non-defining and "that" cannot be used.

Gerunds, Infinitives & Other Key Structures

📌 Verb + Gerund (-ing)
  • Many verbs are always followed by the -ing form, never "to + verb."
  • Typically describe enjoyment, avoidance, suggestion, or completion.
  • No single logical rule — must be memorised as a group.
  • Common ones: enjoy, avoid, suggest, consider, mind, finish, deny, admit, recommend, imagine, risk, postpone.
She enjoys reading historical novels.
He denied taking the missing documents.
Have you considered applying for the scholarship?
They postponed announcing the results until next week.
Would you mind closing the window?
📌 Verb + Infinitive (to + base verb)
  • Other verbs are always followed by "to + verb."
  • Often describe decisions, intentions, promises, hopes, future-oriented actions.
  • Common ones: decide, agree, promise, refuse, manage, afford, hope, expect, plan, offer, threaten, pretend.
They decided to postpone the meeting.
She refused to sign the agreement.
We can't afford to lose this client.
He promised to call as soon as he landed.
She pretended not to notice the mistake.
📌 Verbs That Change Meaning: Gerund vs Infinitive
  • A small group of verbs (remember, forget, stop, try, regret, mean, go on) change MEANING depending on -ing vs "to + verb."
  • Frequent exam trap — both forms are grammatically correct, but only one fits the intended meaning.
I remember locking the door. (a memory of doing it)
I remembered to lock the door. (didn't forget to do this task)
She stopped to answer the phone. (paused one activity to do another)
She stopped answering his calls. (no longer answers)
He tried to open the jar, but it was too tight. (attempted, with effort)
He tried opening the window for some fresh air. (experimented, to see the result)
📌 Used to / Be used to / Get used to
  • used to + base verb = a past habit, no longer true.
  • be used to + noun/-ing = already familiar/accustomed (a state).
  • get used to + noun/-ing = becoming familiar over time (a process).
I used to live in a small village. (past habit, not now)
She's used to working long hours. (already familiar — a state)
He's getting used to the new schedule. (adapting — a process)
✗ wrong I am used to live in a small village. (needs noun/-ing, not base verb)
📌 Causative Verbs: have/get something done
  • Used when someone ELSE performs an action for you.
  • Structure: have/get + object + past participle.
  • "Get" is slightly more informal, sometimes implying more effort/persuasion.
I had my car repaired last week.
She wants to get her hair cut before the event.
We had the house painted over the summer.
📌 Quantifiers: few/a few, little/a little, much/many
  • few/little (without "a") = negative tone — almost none, not enough.
  • a few/a little (with "a") = neutral/positive tone — some, a small but sufficient amount.
  • many/few for countable nouns; much/little for uncountable nouns.
He has few friends here. (almost none — negative)
He has a few friends here. (some — fine)
There's little hope of finishing on time. (almost no hope)
There's a little time left — let's hurry but we can make it.
📌 Articles: a / an / the / (no article)
  • a/an = general, non-specific, singular noun (often first mention).
  • the = specific noun — already mentioned, unique, or clear from context.
  • (no article) = general plural nouns or abstract concepts.
I saw a dog in the park. The dog was barking loudly.
(no article) Education is important for everyone.
The Eiffel Tower is in Paris. (unique landmark)
📌 Phrasal Verbs Frequently Tested
  • Carry meanings completely different from their individual words — learn as whole units.
come up with = think of an idea → "She came up with a brilliant solution."
put off = postpone → "They put off the decision until next month."
look into = investigate → "The police are looking into the incident."
bring about = cause → "The reform brought about major changes."
carry out = perform/conduct → "Researchers carried out a detailed study."
set up = establish/arrange → "They set up a new department last year."
💡 Exam tip: When a verb is followed by a blank, first check whether that verb is normally followed by -ing or "to + verb" — this short list of "fixed partners" is one of the fastest, highest-payoff things to memorise.

Vocabulary (Part 1): Tone, Collocations & Idioms

📌 Word Tone: Positive, Negative, or Neutral
  • Every word carries an emotional "tone" — even words with similar definitions feel different.
  • Recognising tone is one of the fastest ways to eliminate wrong answers.
  • Contrast connectors ("although," "despite," "but," "however") almost always signal a SHIFT in tone between two parts of a sentence.
"Even though the plan looked promising, it turned out to be a complete disaster." (positive setup → negative outcome)
"Although the meeting started badly, it ended on a surprisingly positive note." (negative setup → positive outcome)
"The new policy was warmly welcomed by staff." (clearly positive)
"The new policy was met with fierce resistance." (clearly negative)
📌 Collocations — Words That Travel Together
  • Word combinations that native speakers use together naturally, even when other combinations seem logically possible.
  • Often no grammatical "rule" — must be learned as fixed pairs through exposure.
  • A wrong collocation, while understandable, simply "sounds wrong" to a fluent speaker.
make a decision (not "take")  |  reach an agreement  |  raise awareness  |  play a role
strong coffee (not "powerful")  |  heavy traffic  |  tight schedule  |  high expectations
meet a deadline  |  keep a promise  |  break a record  |  face a challenge
The new policy raised awareness about recycling across the city.
Despite the tight schedule, the team managed to meet the deadline.
📌 Idiomatic Phrases That Often Appear in Exams
  • Carry a meaning very different from their individual words.
  • Cannot be understood literally — must be memorised as whole expressions.
  • Test genuine familiarity with English, not just dictionary knowledge.
stay afloat = survive financially → "The small shop struggled to stay afloat after the mall opened."
fall behind = lag, fail to keep pace → "He started to fall behind in his studies after missing two weeks."
bring about = cause something → "The new law is expected to bring about major changes."
come up with = think of an idea → "The team finally came up with a solution."
put off = postpone → "They put off the announcement until next week."
look into = investigate → "Authorities are looking into the cause of the accident."
get away with = avoid punishment for something wrong → "He got away with arriving late every day."
run out of = have no more of something → "We've run out of time to finish this properly."
📌 Synonyms vs Near-Synonyms — Small Differences Matter
  • Similar-meaning words can differ in INTENSITY (how strong) or FORMALITY (how casual/professional).
  • Choosing a "near-synonym" with the wrong intensity is one of the most common traps.
  • The word isn't wrong in general — just wrong for THIS specific sentence.
angry (neutral) → furious (very strong) → annoyed (mild)
important (neutral) → crucial/vital (extreme) → minor (opposite — small)
big (neutral) → enormous/massive (very large) → tiny (opposite)
"The deadline is crucial — missing it could cost the company the entire contract." (needs the strong word)
💡 Exam tip: Even if you don't recognise a specific word, the overall emotional direction of the sentence (positive or negative) can still point you toward the correct answer — eliminate options with the opposite tone first.

Vocabulary (Part 2): Word Building & Register

📌 Elimination Strategy: Remove the Clearly Wrong Options First
  • In five-option vocabulary questions, usually two choices have nothing to do with the context.
  • Identify and eliminate these immediately.
  • This narrows your focus to the remaining three, saves time, and reduces careless mistakes.
📌 Prefixes — Guessing Meaning From Word Beginnings
  • Prefixes typically add negation, repetition, excess, or direction to the base word.
over- = too much → "overestimate," "overworked"
under- = too little → "underpaid," "underestimate"
mis- = wrongly/badly → "misunderstand," "misinterpret"
re- = again → "rebuild," "reconsider"
dis- = not/opposite → "disagree," "dishonest"
im-/in-/un- = not → "impossible," "incomplete," "unhappy"
"The new instructions were so confusing that most employees misunderstood them."
📌 Suffixes — Guessing Word Type and Meaning
  • Often change a word's grammatical category (noun, adjective, verb).
  • Add specific shades of meaning.
  • Help identify whether a missing word should be a noun, adjective, or verb.
-less = without → "harmless," "careless" (adjective)
-ful = full of → "powerful," "successful" (adjective)
-able/-ible = capable of being → "manageable," "flexible" (adjective)
-tion/-sion = verb → noun → "decision," "explanation" (noun)
-ness = adjective → noun → "happiness," "awareness" (noun)
-ify/-ise/-ize = noun/adjective → verb → "simplify," "modernise" (verb)
📌 Formal vs Informal Vocabulary
  • Exam reading passages and formal letters/reports use a formal register.
  • Recognising formal equivalents of everyday words helps with vocabulary AND translation/rephrasing questions.
  • Exam answer choices often test whether you can match the correct formality level.
get → obtain / acquire (formal)
find out → determine / discover (formal)
a lot of → numerous / considerable (formal)
show → demonstrate / indicate (formal)
ask for → request (formal)
"Researchers determined that the results were statistically significant."
📌 Confusable Word Pairs
  • Some words look or sound similar but have distinctly different meanings.
  • Frequently tested because choosing the wrong one is an easy, common mistake even for advanced learners.
affect (verb — to influence) vs effect (noun — a result) → "The weather affected our plans, and the effect was a delayed flight."
economic (relating to the economy) vs economical (cost-saving) → "The government announced new economic policies." / "This car is very economical on fuel."
sensible (good judgement) vs sensitive (easily affected emotionally) → "It was a sensible decision." / "He's quite sensitive about criticism."
💡 Exam tip: If two options look like the same word with different endings (economic/economical, sensible/sensitive), this is almost always a deliberate trap — check the suffix carefully before choosing.

Translation (Part 1): Structure & Negation

📌 Identify the Structure Before You Translate
  • The first step is NOT translating word by word.
  • Recognise the grammatical structure first: conditional? Passive? Comparison? Relative clause?
  • Once the structure is clear, finding the matching equivalent is far easier.
  • You're less likely to be misled by an option that "sounds similar" but uses the wrong grammar.
"Had the team prepared better, they would have won the competition." → recognise Third Conditional before translating any word.
"The proposal, which took months to draft, was rejected immediately." → recognise the non-defining relative clause first.
📌 Watch Out for Negation Traps
  • One of the most common tricks: flipping a positive statement into a negative one, or the reverse.
  • Especially dangerous because the rest of the sentence may look almost identical.
  • Always check words like "never," "rarely," "hardly," "barely," "no," "not" carefully — in both the original and each option.
Original: "The plan was never approved by the board."
Trap: "Plan, yönetim kurulu tarafından onaylandı." (meaning reversed!)
Correct: "Plan, yönetim kurulu tarafından hiçbir zaman onaylanmadı."
Original: "Few people doubted her ability to lead the project."
Trap: "Çoğu insan onun projeyi yönetme yeteneğinden şüphe duydu." (reverses "few" into "most")
📌 Preserve the Degree of Certainty
  • The difference between possibility ("might/may/could") and certainty ("will/must/definitely") must always be preserved.
  • Translating a possibility as a certainty (or vice versa) changes the entire meaning, even if other words are correct.
Original: "Experts predict that prices will rise." → confident prediction.
Trap: "Bazı uzmanlara göre fiyatlar belki artabilir." (certainty weakened)
Original: "The committee might consider the proposal." → only a possibility.
Trap: "Komite, teklifi değerlendirecektir." (certainty wrongly added)
📌 Preserve Tense and Timeframe
  • Past, present, and future must stay consistent between original and translation.
  • A translation shifting "already happened" to "will happen," or "ongoing" to "finished," is incorrect — even with accurate vocabulary.
Original: "The company has been struggling financially for two years." (ongoing, still true)
Trap: "Şirket iki yıl önce mali olarak zor günler geçirmişti." (implies it's over — wrong)
💡 Exam tip: Before reading the answer options, try forming your OWN rough translation of the original sentence in your head first — then compare it to the options. This prevents you from being misled by a well-written but incorrect option.

Translation (Part 2): Logic, Tone & Completeness

📌 Don't Reverse Cause and Effect
  • Some wrong options swap the cause and the result.
  • This error can be subtle — individual words may be translated correctly, only the LOGICAL DIRECTION is wrong.
  • Always ask: "in the original sentence, what causes what?"
Original: "Poor planning led to the project's failure." → poor planning (cause) → failure (result).
Trap: "Projenin başarısızlığı kötü planlamaya yol açtı." (reversed)
Original: "Heavy rainfall caused the river to flood."
Trap: "Nehrin taşması yoğun yağmura neden oldu." (reversed)
📌 Don't Add or Remove Information
  • A correct translation never adds information not in the original.
  • It also never silently drops information that WAS there.
  • Be suspicious of: an extra specific detail (a number, a name, an exaggeration), or a detail clearly stated but missing.
Original: "The factory reduced emissions by 20% last year."
Trap: "Fabrika geçen yıl emisyonlarını tamamen ortadan kaldırdı." ("20%" replaced with exaggerated "completely")
Original: "The mayor briefly addressed the crowd before leaving."
Trap: "Belediye başkanı kalabalığa uzun bir konuşma yaptı." (reverses "briefly")
📌 Keep the Tone and Formality Level
  • A formal, measured sentence should be translated into similarly formal, measured language.
  • Don't let an informal or neutral sentence become dramatic or exaggerated in translation.
  • Harder to spot — the core facts may be accurate; only the EMOTIONAL INTENSITY has changed.
Original: "The board expressed concern about the budget." (formal, measured)
Trap: "Yönetim kurulu bütçe konusunda çok endişeliydi ve paniğe kapıldı." ("panic" much stronger than "concern")
Original: "Sales declined slightly in the third quarter."
Trap: "Satışlar üçüncü çeyrekte tamamen çöktü." ("collapsed completely" far stronger)
📌 Multi-Clause Sentences: Translate Each Part Correctly
  • Longer sentences often have two or three clauses joined by connectors (although, because, and, but).
  • A wrong option might translate ONE clause correctly while getting another wrong.
  • Always check every part of a long sentence individually, not just the overall "feel."
Original: "Although the budget was limited, the team managed to complete the project on time."
Check each part separately: (1) budget was limited; (2) team completed it; (3) on time. All three must match.
💡 Exam tip: For every translation option, ask three questions in order: Does it have the same FACTS? The same TONE/intensity? The same CAUSE-EFFECT direction? If any answer is "no," eliminate that option.
✏️ Check Yourself

2) "The CEO briefly mentioned the merger during the call." cümlesinin en doğru çevirisi hangisidir?

Page 14 / 17 — Rephrasing

Rephrasing (Part 1): Word Overlap & Sequencing

📌 Same Words ≠ Same Meaning
  • An option isn't automatically correct just because it repeats many words from the original.
  • An option isn't automatically wrong just because it uses different vocabulary.
  • Always focus on whether the MEANING matches, not the words.
  • Test writers deliberately create high-word-overlap options with distorted meaning, to catch students who scan for familiar words.
Original: "The manager rarely praises his team."
Trap (same words, wrong meaning): "The manager always praises his team rarely." (nonsensical)
Correct (different words, same meaning): "The manager hardly ever says anything positive about his team."
📌 "It wasn't until..." and "Only after..." Structures
  • Describe something that happened ONLY AFTER a specific event — never before, not at the same time.
  • Getting the sequence exactly right is essential.
  • Designed to test whether you can track time order through a rephrased sentence.
"It wasn't until he failed twice that he finally succeeded." = "Only after failing twice did he succeed."
Wrong: "He succeeded before failing twice." (sequence reversed)
"It wasn't until the storm passed that the ships could leave the harbour." = "Only after the storm passed could the ships leave."
"Not until she apologised did he agree to speak with her again." = "He only agreed to speak with her again after she apologised."
📌 No sooner... than / Hardly... when — Immediate Sequence
  • Describe one event happening immediately after another — almost simultaneously.
  • Both require inversion (auxiliary before subject) at the start of a sentence.
No sooner had she sat down than the phone rang. (= as soon as she sat down...)
Hardly had he finished speaking when the audience began applauding.
📌 Don't Change the Degree (hardly any / most / all)
  • Quantity words ("hardly any," "barely," "almost none") are very different from "all," "most," "the majority of."
  • If a rephrased option shifts this degree — even slightly — it changes the factual claim.
Original: "Hardly any of the staff understood the new policy."
Wrong: "Most of the staff understood the new policy." (degree completely reversed)
Correct: "Very few of the staff understood the new policy."
Original: "The majority of customers were satisfied with the service."
Wrong: "A small number of customers were satisfied." (degree reversed)
💡 Exam tip: Circle (mentally or literally) every quantity/frequency word in the original sentence — hardly, rarely, most, all, never, always — and verify the chosen option keeps the exact same strength.

2) "It wasn't until the manager apologised that the customer calmed down." cümlesine en yakın anlamlı seçenek hangisidir?

Page 15 / 17 — Rephrasing

Rephrasing (Part 2): Conditionals & Logical Relationships

📌 Real or Hypothetical? Check the Conditional Type
  • The most important question: did the event actually happen in reality?
  • Third Conditional ALWAYS describes something that did NOT happen.
  • A correct rephrased version must describe the real, opposite outcome that DID happen — not pretend the hypothetical was real.
"If the team had practised more, they would have won." → the team did NOT win.
Correct: "The team lost because they hadn't practised enough."
Wrong: "The team practised a lot and won the match." (treats the hypothetical as real)
"Had she known about the traffic, she would have left earlier." → she did NOT leave earlier.
Correct: "She left late because she didn't know about the traffic."
📌 Cause, Result, Contrast, or Addition? Preserve the Logical Relationship
  • A correctly rephrased sentence must preserve the EXACT logical relationship of the original.
  • Replacing "because" (cause) with "despite" (contrast) completely reverses the logic.
  • Identify the connector's FUNCTION first: explaining WHY, or showing an unexpected contrast?
Original: "Because of the heavy snow, the flight was delayed." (cause → result)
Wrong: "Despite the heavy snow, the flight was delayed." (implies snow should have prevented delay — wrong logic)
Original: "Despite the heavy snow, all flights departed on schedule." (contrast)
Wrong: "Because of the heavy snow, all flights departed on schedule." (illogical)
📌 Rephrasing Comparisons
  • When a sentence involves a comparison ("more...than," "as...as," "the same as," "different from"), the rephrased version must keep the SAME relative ranking.
  • Don't accidentally swap which one is bigger, better, or more important.
Original: "This year's profits were significantly higher than last year's."
Correct: "Profits increased significantly compared to last year."
Wrong: "This year's profits were lower than last year's." (direction reversed)
📌 Rephrasing Passive ↔ Active Without Changing Meaning
  • A sentence can often be rephrased between active and passive while keeping the exact same meaning.
  • The subject and object roles must be correctly swapped (original subject → "by" agent; original object → new subject).
Original (active): "The committee rejected the proposal."
Correct (passive): "The proposal was rejected by the committee."
💡 Exam tip: After choosing your answer, briefly explain to yourself, in your own words, why each of the other options is wrong — naming the specific error (reversed cause, wrong tense, changed degree) builds a habit that prevents careless mistakes.

2) "Because the bridge was closed, traffic was diverted through the city centre." cümlesine en yakın anlamlı seçenek hangisidir?

3) "This year's sales were significantly higher than last year's." cümlesine en yakın anlamlı seçenek hangisidir?

Page 16 / 17 — Dialogue Completion

Dialogue Completion — What to Watch For

📌 Follow the Flow and Emotional Tone of the Conversation
  • The correct answer must be a natural continuation of the conversation.
  • Should not suddenly change the established emotional tone (worried, happy, confused, frustrated).
  • Should not jump to a completely unrelated topic.
  • Predict in your own words what kind of response makes sense, then match the closest option.
A: "I'm worried the presentation went badly." → B's reply should stay connected to this worry — not lunch plans.
A: "I just got some amazing news!" → B's reply should match the excited, positive tone.
📌 Eliminate Options That Contradict the Previous Line
  • If a speaker clearly stated something, the next line should not directly deny it without a clear reason.
  • Watch for options that conflict with something already established earlier.
A: "I'm still not sure if I should take the job."
B (wrong): "Actually, I've already accepted three job offers this year." (contradicts A's uncertainty)
B (correct): "That's understandable — it's a big decision to make."
📌 If a Question or Suggestion Is Made, the Reply Must Address It
  • When one speaker asks a question or makes a suggestion, the reply should respond directly.
  • A reply that ignores the question and changes the subject is almost always wrong.
A: "Why don't we reschedule the meeting for Friday?"
B (correct): "Friday works for me — I'll let the others know."
B (wrong): "I had pasta for lunch today." (unrelated)
A: "Could you send me the file again? I think I lost it."
B (correct): "Sure, no problem — I'll send it right now."
📌 Formal or Informal? Match the Register
  • Look at the context: workplace/customer service/academic (formal) vs friends/family (informal).
  • The correct answer should match this level of formality consistently.
  • A sudden formal ↔ casual shift within the same dialogue usually signals a wrong option.
formal Customer service: "I completely understand your concern, and I'll resolve this for you right away."
informal Between friends: "Oh no, that's rough! Want to talk about it?"
✗ mismatched Customer service: "Yeah, that totally sucks, I feel you." (too informal)
📌 Be Cautious of Extreme or Absolute Statements
  • Phrases like "I will never...", "Nothing can be done...", "Everyone agrees..." are usually WRONG answers.
  • Real conversations are rarely this absolute or dramatic.
  • Exam writers often include one "too extreme" option as a distractor.
"I will never trust anyone at this company again." (too extreme)
More natural: "It's going to take some time before I trust the new system again."
📌 Pay Attention to Pronoun and Reference Consistency
  • The correct reply must use pronouns/references that logically connect to what was just said.
  • If Speaker A mentions "my sister," Speaker B's reply should refer back correctly ("her"/"your sister") — not introduce a confusing reference.
💡 Exam tip: Try reading the full dialogue aloud (or silently) with each option inserted — whichever answer sounds completely "natural" in the flow, and which sounds slightly "odd," even if you can't explain exactly why? This test often leads you straight to the right choice.

Situational Sentences — What to Watch For

📌 First Identify the Situation, Then the Purpose
  • The situation description is always given first, in plain language.
  • First job: identify exactly WHO is speaking to WHOM, and the relationship (friend, stranger, boss, customer).
  • Second job: identify the PURPOSE/GOAL — apologising, praising, polite request, complaining, advice, declining?
  • Misreading the relationship or the goal is the most common source of error.
📌 Polite Request Patterns
  • Standard patterns soften a request and show respect.
  • Essential whenever the situation calls for politeness — strangers, superiors, professional contexts.
Would you mind closing the window?  |  Could you possibly send that file again?  |  I wonder if you could help me with this.
Would it be possible to reschedule our meeting?  |  Do you think you could lower your voice a little?
Direct command ("Close the window now.") is usually not appropriate for a polite request.
📌 Apology and Responsibility Patterns
  • The strongest apology answers usually contain up to three elements together:
  • (1) a clear apology
  • (2) acknowledging responsibility
  • (3) an offer of a solution/fix/compensation
  • An answer that only apologises, without addressing the problem, is often weaker than one combining all three.
"I'm sorry about the mix-up — let me sort it out for you right away."
"I apologise for the delay. I'll have the report ready by tomorrow morning."
"I'm really sorry I forgot your birthday — let me make it up to you this weekend."
📌 Praise and Compliment Sentences
  • Should be sincere, clear, and warm — never dismissive, conditional, or backhanded.
  • Watch for options that seem complimentary but include a hidden criticism or condition.
"This is truly impressive work — you should be proud of what you've achieved."
"I guess it's okay, considering you didn't have much time." (backhanded)
"You did an excellent job handling that difficult client."
📌 Giving Advice or Warning Politely
  • Language should be softened — "should," "might want to," "it would probably be a good idea to."
  • Avoid blunt commands, especially with someone who isn't a close friend.
"You might want to double-check those numbers before sending the report."
"It would probably be a good idea to leave a bit earlier, given the traffic."
📌 Politely Declining or Disagreeing
  • Acknowledge the other person's point first.
  • Then express disagreement/refusal gently, often with a brief reason.
"That's a fair point, but I think we should consider the budget first."
"Thank you for the offer, but I won't be able to make it this time."
📌 Common Types of Wrong Options to Avoid
  • 🚫 A sentence completely unrelated to the described situation
  • 🚫 A sentence with a rude, sarcastic, or disrespectful tone where politeness was required
  • 🚫 A sentence that does the exact OPPOSITE of what's needed (criticising instead of praising, accepting instead of declining)
  • 🚫 A sentence about the right topic, but addressed to the wrong person or wrong point of view
💡 Exam tip: The situation description almost always states the goal directly using a clear verb (apologise, request, praise, complain, advise, decline). Underline that verb first — it is the single biggest clue to the correct answer.
