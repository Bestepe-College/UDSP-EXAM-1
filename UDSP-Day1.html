8
By the time the rescue team arrived, the stranded hikers - - - - in the mountains for over thirty hours.
✓ Doğru Cevap & Açıklama
Doğru Cevap: D) had been waiting
"By the time + past simple" triggers the Past Perfect Continuous tense for the action that had been ongoing before a past point. The hikers' waiting started before the team arrived and was still ongoing — requiring "had been waiting" (Past Perfect Continuous).
A) are waiting — şu an bekliyorlar (Present Continuous — wrong tense for a past context)
D) had been waiting ✓ — bekliyorlardı (bitmemiş, geçmiş öncesi süreç) (Past Perfect Continuous)
C) have waited — beklemiş oldular (Present Perfect — cannot be used with past reference "arrived")
B) were waiting — bekliyorlardı (Past Continuous — no sense of duration before a past point)
E) will have waited — beklemiş olacaklar (Future Perfect — wrong tense entirely)
9
If the students - - - - more carefully, they would not have made so many mistakes in the exam.
✓ Doğru Cevap & Açıklama
Doğru Cevap: B) had read
The result clause contains "would not have made" — this is the Third Conditional (unreal past). The if-clause of Type 3 requires Past Perfect: "If + had + V3". This refers to a past situation that did not happen.
A) read — okumuş olsaydılar (simple past — Type 2 conditional, not matching result clause)
B) had read ✓ — okumuş olsaydılar (Past Perfect — correct for Type 3)
C) would read — okurlar (modal — cannot go in the if-clause)
D) have read — okumuşlardır (Present Perfect — wrong tense)
E) were reading — okuyorlardı (Past Continuous — Type 2, not Type 3)
10
The children enjoyed - - - - stories by their grandmother every evening before bed.
✓ Doğru Cevap & Açıklama
Doğru Cevap: E) hearing
"Enjoy" is a verb that must be followed by a gerund (-ing form), not an infinitive. Key verbs followed by gerunds: enjoy, avoid, consider, suggest, finish, deny, practise, miss, keep, mind.
A) to hear — duymak için (infinitive — enjoy does NOT take to-infinitive)
B) hear — duymak (bare infinitive — incorrect after enjoy)
C) heard — duydu/duyulmuş (past/past participle — grammatically impossible here)
E) hearing ✓ — duymak, dinlemek (-ing/gerund — required after "enjoy")
D) to have heard — duymuş olmak (perfect infinitive — incorrect after enjoy)
11
This is the most fascinating novel - - - - I have ever read in my entire life.
✓ Doğru Cevap & Açıklama
Doğru Cevap: B) which / that
The antecedent is "novel" — a thing, not a person. Use "which" or "that" for things/objects. Also note: after superlatives ("most fascinating"), "that" is preferred in relative clauses. "Who/whom" are for people; "whose" is for possession; "where" is for places.
A) who — kişiler için kullanılır (for people — novel is a thing)
C) whom — kişiler için nesne hali (object for people — wrong)
B) which / that ✓ — nesneler ve varlıklar için (for things — correct)
D) whose — kimin, neyin (possessive relative — no possession here)
E) where — yer zarfı (for places — novel is not a place)
12
You - - - - wear a helmet when riding a bicycle; it is the law and can save your life.
✓ Doğru Cevap & Açıklama
Doğru Cevap: C) must
"Must" expresses strong obligation or necessity, especially legal requirements. The clue "it is the law" confirms this is not optional — it's compulsory. "Must" (zorunluluk) is the only modal that conveys legal obligation in the present.
A) might — belki, ihtimal (possibility — far too weak for a law)
B) could — yapabilirsin (ability/possibility — not obligation)
C) must ✓ — zorunlu, mecburi (strong necessity/legal obligation)
D) would — conditional/istek (habitual past or willingness — not obligation)
E) should have — yapmalıydın (past regret — wrong tense, implies it wasn't done)
13
The new hospital - - - - in the city centre last year, and it has already treated thousands of patients.
✓ Doğru Cevap & Açıklama
Doğru Cevap: A) was built
The time marker "last year" signals Simple Past tense. The hospital is the object of building (it doesn't build itself), so we need passive voice: "was built." Passive Simple Past = was/were + past participle.
C) is built — inşa edilmektedir (Present Simple Passive — "last year" requires past)
B) builds — inşa ediyor (active, present — hospital cannot build)
A) was built ✓ — inşa edildi (Past Simple Passive — correct)
D) had built — inşa etmişti (Past Perfect Active — wrong voice & tense)
E) has been built — inşa edilmiştir (Present Perfect Passive — clashes with "last year")
14
Neither the manager - - - - the employees were informed about the sudden change in company policy.
✓ Doğru Cevap & Açıklama
Doğru Cevap: D) nor
"Neither...nor" is the correct correlative conjunction pair. It is used to negate two subjects or ideas together. "Neither A nor B" means "not A and not B." The pair "neither...or" is incorrect in standard English.
A) or — veya (used with "either", not "neither")
B) and — ve (additive, positive conjunction — wrong with "neither")
D) nor ✓ — ne de (correct pair: neither...nor)
D) but — ama, ancak (contrast conjunction — wrong pair)
E) yet — henüz / ama (time or contrast — wrong pair)

The Psychology of Colour

Colour is far more than a visual experience — it plays a significant role in shaping human emotions and behaviour. Psychologists and designers have long studied how different colours affect our mood, decision-making, and even our physical responses. For instance, the colour red has been shown to increase heart rate and create a sense of urgency, which is why it is commonly used in warning signs and sale advertisements. Blue, on the other hand, is associated with calmness and trust, making it a favourite choice for banks and healthcare institutions.

Research has also found that colour can influence performance. A study conducted at several universities revealed that students who took tests in rooms painted red scored significantly lower than those in rooms with neutral colours. Scientists believe this is because red triggers a stress response in the brain, which interferes with concentration. Meanwhile, green, which is often linked to nature and balance, has been shown to enhance creativity and reduce fatigue.

However, it is important to note that colour perception is not entirely universal. Cultural background plays a major role in how people interpret colours. White, for example, symbolises purity and peace in many Western cultures, whereas in several East Asian cultures it is associated with mourning and death. Similarly, while purple represents royalty in Europe, it carries different connotations in other parts of the world.

Despite these cultural differences, some emotional responses to colour appear to be consistent across cultures. Most people, regardless of their background, respond positively to bright, warm colours in social environments and prefer cooler tones in spaces designed for focus or relaxation. Understanding colour psychology can therefore be a powerful tool for architects, marketers, and educators who wish to design environments that optimise human well-being and performance.
