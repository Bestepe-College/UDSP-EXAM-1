GRAMMAR

8

A) Geliştirmiş olan (aktif — yanlış)
B) Geliştirmiş olmak (infinitive — yanlış)

12
The policy changes were so poorly communicated - - - - most employees had no idea what was expected of them.
Doğru Cevap: E
A) her ne kadar (zıtlık)
B) rağmen (zıtlık)
C) bu yüzden (bağımsız cümle başlatır)
D) olmadıkça
E) o kadar...ki (so...that) ✓
"So + adjective + THAT + result clause" — fixed structure. The poor communication caused the employees' confusion.
13
She admitted - - - - the project deadline without informing the rest of the team.
Doğru Cevap: D
A) kaçırmayı (to-inf. — yanlış)
B) eksik olmak (to be + -ing adj — 'admit' sonrası yanlış)
C) kaçırıyor olmayı (süreç — yanlış)
D) kaçırmış olmayı (having+V3) ✓
E) kaçırmak (base — yanlış)
"Admit" is followed by -ing or "having + V3". "Having missed" (D) best emphasises that the missing happened BEFORE the admission. "To miss" (A) is wrong — 'admit' never takes a bare infinitive. "To be missing" (B) = a state adjective phrase, not used here. "Miss" (E) is a bare infinitive — also wrong.
14
The researchers insisted that all participants - - - - fully informed of their right to withdraw from the study at any time.
Doğru Cevap: E
A) olur (Present Simple)
B) oldu (Past Simple)
C) olmuş olan (participial)
D) olarak (gerund)
E) olsun (subjunctive base form) ✓
After insist/demand/require/suggest: "insist that + subject + BASE VERB" (subjunctive).

Passage 1: The Hidden Cost of Sleep Deprivation

In contemporary society, sleep is increasingly treated as a luxury rather than a biological necessity. Many people routinely cut their sleep short to accommodate professional demands, social commitments, or screen time, often consoling themselves with the belief that they can "catch up" on rest at the weekend. However, researchers now understand that sleep debt — the cumulative shortfall between the sleep an individual needs and the sleep they actually get — cannot simply be repaid in a single extended rest. Its effects accumulate gradually and are not always immediately obvious to the individual experiencing them.

At the neurological level, sleep deprivation has a particularly pronounced effect on the prefrontal cortex, the region of the brain responsible for executive functions such as rational decision-making, impulse control, and forward planning. When this region is impaired by insufficient rest, individuals become less able to weigh risks carefully, control impulses, or think ahead — consequences that extend well beyond mere tiredness. Research conducted at leading universities has consistently found that individuals operating on six hours of sleep or fewer perform cognitive tasks at a level comparable to those who have been awake for twenty-four hours straight, yet many of these individuals report feeling only mildly tired.

The long-term health implications are equally concerning. Sustained sleep deprivation has been linked to elevated risks of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and a weakened immune system. It also has well-documented effects on mental health, with chronic sleep loss associated with increased rates of anxiety and depression. What makes these consequences particularly serious is that they develop silently — a person may not recognise the extent to which their health has been compromised until significant harm has already occurred.

Passage 2: Vertical Farming and the Future of Urban Food

As global urbanisation accelerates and climate change places increasing pressure on conventional agricultural systems, vertical farming has emerged as one of the more promising — if still contested — solutions to the challenge of feeding growing city populations. Unlike traditional farming, which depends on large areas of arable land, favourable weather conditions, and vast quantities of water, vertical farms grow crops in stacked, climate-controlled indoor environments using artificial LED lighting and hydroponic or aeroponic systems. These facilities can use up to 95% less water than conventional fields, produce food year-round independent of seasonal variation, and be located within or adjacent to the urban centres they serve.

Proponents argue that vertical farming could help reduce the environmental footprint of food production by shortening supply chains, minimising the need for pesticides, and freeing up agricultural land for ecological restoration. Pilot projects in cities including Singapore, Tokyo, and Dubai have demonstrated that the model is technically viable and capable of producing high yields of leafy greens, herbs, and certain fruits. The principal barrier to its widespread adoption, however, remains the substantial initial investment required — in terms of both infrastructure and energy consumption — which currently makes vertically farmed produce considerably more expensive than conventionally grown alternatives.

Critics also point out that while vertical farming may work well for certain high-value crops, it remains largely impractical for staple crops such as wheat, rice, and maize, which require large volumes and low margins to be economically viable. Whether vertical farming can move beyond a niche supplement to the food supply and become a genuinely transformative technology will depend largely on whether ongoing advances in renewable energy and automation can bring costs down to a level that makes the model commercially competitive at scale.
