✓ Doğru Cevap & Açıklama

"By the time + past simple" is a classic trigger for Past Perfect, indicating an action completed before another past event. The fire had already spread before the firefighters' arrival — Past Perfect is required.

B) are spreading — yayılıyor (Present Continuous — wrong tense entirely)

✓ Doğru Cevap & Açıklama
Doğru Cevap: E) must be finished
"Insisted that...must be" expresses a strong obligation in reported/indirect speech. Since the report itself doesn't finish anything (it's finished BY someone), the passive form "must be finished" is needed, fitting the strict deadline implied by "no exceptions allowed."
A) finished — bitti (simple past — doesn't show the required obligation)
B) finishes — bitirir (active present — wrong, the report can't finish itself)
C) will be finished — bitirilecek (future passive — possible but weaker than expressing obligation)
D) has finished — bitirmiştir (active present perfect — wrong voice)
E) must be finished ✓ — bitirilmelidir (passive obligation — matches "insisted" + "no exceptions")
10

✓ Doğru Cevap & Açıklama
Doğru Cevap: D) Despite
"Despite" is followed by a noun phrase (not a clause) and expresses concession. "Despite the heavy traffic" is grammatically correct since "the heavy traffic" is a noun phrase. "Although" would require a full clause ("Although there was heavy traffic").
A) Although — rağmen, her ne kadar (needs a clause with subject+verb, not noun phrase)
B) Because of — yüzünden, nedeniyle (cause — wrong, contradicts "still managed")
C) Even if — eğer...olsa bile (conditional, not concession with noun phrase)
D) Despite ✓ — rağmen (correctly takes noun phrase)
E) Unless — eğer...olmazsa (conditional negative — wrong meaning)
11
The lecture, - - - - covered three major theories, lasted almost two hours.
✓ Doğru Cevap & Açıklama
Doğru Cevap: A) which
"Which" is the correct relative pronoun for a non-restrictive clause referring to a thing ("the lecture"). "Who" and "whom" are for people; "whose" shows possession; "where" is for places.
A) which ✓ — ki o (relative pronoun for things, non-restrictive)
B) who — kim (for people, not lectures)
C) whose — kimin (possessive — no possession indicated here)
D) whom — kimi (object pronoun for people, not things)
E) where — nerede (for locations, not the lecture itself)

Doğru Cevap: C) might have been avoided
"If + had + past participle" is the standard Third Conditional structure for an unreal past situation. The main clause needs "modal + have + past participle" — here in passive form since the failure was avoided BY testing: "might have been avoided."
A) would happen — gerçekleşirdi (wrong tense, not perfect form)
B) will be avoided — kaçınılacak (future — wrong for hypothetical past)
C) might have been avoided ✓ — önlenebilirdi (correct Third Conditional passive)
D) is avoided — kaçınılıyor (present — wrong tense entirely)
E) avoids — kaçınır (present simple active — grammatically wrong subject relationship)
13
The professor is known - - - - one of the leading experts in molecular biology worldwide.

Doğru Cevap: B) to be
"Be known to be + adjective/noun" is a fixed structure meaning "be recognised as having a certain quality/status." "The professor is known to be one of the leading experts" is the standard form, distinct from "known for + gerund" (known for doing something specific).
A) as being — olarak bilinmek (not the standard fixed pattern here)
B) to be ✓ — olduğu bilinir (correct fixed structure "known to be")

With "neither...nor," the verb agrees with the noun closest to it — here "the employees" (plural), so the verb must be plural: "were." Past tense is needed because of "until it was announced" (past context).
A) was — singular past — wrong because closest noun "employees" is plural
B) is — present tense — wrong, context is past ("was announced")

Passage 1: The Decline of Handwriting

As digital devices become an increasingly central part of daily life, many educators have raised concerns about the declining emphasis on handwriting in schools. In several countries, cursive writing has been removed from the curriculum altogether, replaced by typing lessons that begin as early as the first grade. Students who take notes by hand, for instance, often demonstrate better recall of lecture content than those who type their notes word for word, possibly because handwriting naturally encourages summarising rather than transcribing.

Given these findings, some schools have begun reintroducing handwriting instruction, not as a replacement for digital literacy but as a complementary skill. Educators in these schools argue that students should be equally comfortable with both forms of writing, using each where it is most appropriate — handwriting for deep engagement with material, and typing for speed and efficiency in other contexts.

Passage 2: The Economics of Food Waste

Every year, an estimated one-third of all food produced globally is lost or wasted, a statistic that becomes even more troubling when considered alongside the fact that hundreds of millions of people worldwide suffer from chronic hunger. This paradox has prompted economists and policymakers to examine the issue not merely as a moral failing but as a systemic economic problem with measurable costs at every stage of the supply chain.

In wealthier nations, much of the waste occurs at the consumer level, where confusing date labels often lead people to discard food that remains perfectly safe to eat. A product labelled "best before" indicates peak quality rather than safety, yet many consumers interpret it as an expiration deadline and throw away food prematurely. Retailers also contribute significantly to this problem by rejecting fruits and vegetables that fail to meet strict cosmetic standards, even when the produce is nutritionally identical to "perfect" specimens. In developing countries, by contrast, the bulk of food loss happens earlier in the supply chain, often due to inadequate storage facilities, unreliable transportation, and a lack of refrigeration that allows perishable goods to spoil before reaching markets.

Addressing this issue requires different strategies depending on where the waste occurs. In wealthier nations, public education campaigns about date labels and consumer behaviour have shown some promise, alongside retailer initiatives to sell "imperfect" produce at discounted prices. In developing regions, however, the priority lies in infrastructure investment — building better storage and transport networks that can preserve food quality from farm to market. Some economists argue that solving food waste could be one of the most cost-effective ways to address global hunger, since it would not require producing more food, only preserving what already exists.
